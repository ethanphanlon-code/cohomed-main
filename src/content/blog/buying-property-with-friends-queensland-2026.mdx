## Understanding Co-ownership in Queensland

When purchasing property with friends in Queensland, you'll need to decide on a legal ownership structure. The two main options are **tenants in common** and **joint tenancy**, and they have very different implications for your rights and obligations.

### Joint Tenancy vs Tenants in Common

**Joint Tenancy:**
- All owners have equal rights to the property
- If one owner dies, their share automatically passes to the surviving owners
- Cannot leave the property to beneficiaries in a will
- More suitable for couples or very close relationships

**Tenants in Common:**
- Each owner holds a specific percentage (can be unequal)
- Your share is part of your estate and can be left to beneficiaries
- Better for friend groups with different financial contributions
- More flexible if ownership changes

For most friend groups, **tenants in common** is the recommended structure because it allows unequal ownership splits and protects each person's individual interests.

## Setting Up Your Co-ownership Agreement

The foundation of any successful co-ownership arrangement is a solid agreement. This document should cover:

- **Ownership percentages** and how they relate to financial contributions
- **Expense responsibilities** (mortgage, rates, insurance, maintenance)
- **Decision-making processes** for major changes (renovations, refinancing, selling)
- **What happens if someone wants to exit** the arrangement
- **Dispute resolution procedures**
- **Exit strategies** and how buyouts work

Queensland has specific property laws that govern these arrangements. While a handshake agreement might work between best friends, a legally drafted document protects everyone and prevents misunderstandings down the track.

## Government Schemes Available in Queensland

Queensland offers several schemes that friend groups can take advantage of:

### First Home Owner Grant
- Up to $30,000 for new homes under $750,000
- Each first-time buyer in the group can claim this separately
- Significantly reduces the upfront cost for your group

### Stamp Duty Concessions
- New homes: No stamp duty in Queensland (one of Australia's best schemes)
- Established homes: Reduced rates for first-home buyers
- Groups can stack these savings

### Boost to Buy
- First home buyers can access a government guarantee
- Deposit as low as 2% (government covers up to 30%)
- Available since 2023 specifically for eligible groups

## Financial Planning for Friends

### Creating a Fair Cost Split

Before buying, your group needs to agree on how costs will be divided:

1. **Initial Purchase Costs**
   - Deposit (split according to agreed ownership percentages)
   - Legal fees, inspections, and conveyancing
   - Stamp duty and settlement costs

2. **Ongoing Costs**
   - Mortgage payments (split by ownership %)
   - Council rates and water charges
   - Insurance (building and contents)
   - Maintenance and repairs
   - Body corporate fees (if applicable)

3. **Exit Strategy**
   - What happens when someone buys out their share?
   - How is the property valued?
   - Timeline for the buyout

## Legal Protections You Need

### Must-Have Documents

1. **Co-ownership Agreement** (prepared by a lawyer)
   - Not the same as a mortgage agreement
   - Covers dispute resolution and exit procedures
   - Protects your individual interests

2. **Caveat or Caveat Emptor Registration**
   - Protects your ownership on the title
   - Required for tenants in common arrangements

3. **Insurance Documents**
   - Building insurance (mortgage lender requirement)
   - Contents insurance for shared items
   - Income protection for mortgage payments

### Working with Your Mortgage Broker

Queensland mortgage brokers understand co-ownership arrangements, but not all do. When selecting a broker:

- Ask about their experience with group purchases
- Ensure they understand first-home buyer schemes
- Confirm they can handle unequal ownership percentages
- Get everything in writing

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### 1. Unclear Ownership Percentages
Never leave ownership percentages vague. Even if you're "splitting equally," get it in writing as 25%, 25%, 25%, 25% (for four people).

### 2. Mixing Money Without Documentation
Each contribution should be documented. Bank transfers are preferable to cash payments.

### 3. Relying on Friendship Instead of Agreements
The best friendships are protected by clear agreements. They prevent misunderstandings that could harm relationships.

### 4. Not Considering Exit Scenarios
What happens if someone loses their job? Gets married? Wants to leave? Plan for these scenarios upfront.

### 5. Underestimating Ongoing Costs
Property ownership has hidden costs. Factor in maintenance buffers, interest rate rises, and unexpected repairs.
