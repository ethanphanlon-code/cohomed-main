## What Is the First Home Super Saver Scheme?

The **First Home Super Saver Scheme (FHSS)** is a government program that lets first-home buyers withdraw money from their superannuation to help buy their first property. Introduced in 2017 and enhanced in 2024, it's one of Australia's most valuable first-home buyer benefits.

### Key Features (2026 Update)

As of 2024 reforms, the scheme allows:
- **$50,000 withdrawal limit** per person (up from $35,000)
- **Withdrawals from your own super** only
- **Tax-free contributions** to super specifically for this purpose
- **No capital gains tax** on the growth of contributions withdrawn
- Available for Australian citizens and permanent residents

## How the Scheme Works: Step by Step

### Step 1: Make Voluntary Contributions to Super

You make extra contributions to your superannuation beyond your employer contributions. These are treated as personal income tax deductible contributions.

**Example:**
You earn $60,000 per year and want to save an extra $10,000 for your house deposit:
- Make $10,000 voluntary contribution to your super
- Claim this as a tax deduction (save ~$3,000 in tax)
- Net cost: $7,000 to boost your super by $10,000

### Step 2: Designate Contributions for the FHSS

Tell your super fund which contributions are for the FHSS. These are now tracked separately and eligible for withdrawal.

### Step 3: Withdraw When You're Ready

When you're buying your first property:
- Apply to the ATO to release your FHSS amount
- You can withdraw up to $50,000 or your total FHSS balance, whichever is lower
- The withdrawal is tax-free

### Step 4: Use for Your Purchase

The money is yours to use for:
- Deposit savings
- Stamp duty and legal fees
- Building inspections
- Valuation fees
- Mortgage insurance

**Important:** The funds must be used towards purchasing your first home. Withdrawing for other purposes has tax consequences.

## Co-Buyers and the FHSS: Maximum Impact

For friend groups buying together, the FHSS can be transformative.

### Scenario: Four Friends Buying Together

Let's say four friends want to buy a $600,000 apartment in Queensland:

**Without FHSS:**
- Conventional 20% deposit = $120,000
- Split equally = $30,000 per person
- Difficult to save simultaneously

**With FHSS (All Eligible):**
- Each contributes $10,000/year to super for 3 years = $30,000 each
- Tax savings = $9,000 each (at 30% MTR)
- Each person withdraws $30,000 FHSS + saves $9,000 from tax refunds
- **Total deposit per person = $39,000**
- **Total group deposit = $156,000** (26% of property value)

**Benefits:**
- Lower loan-to-value ratio (LVR) means better interest rates
- No lenders mortgage insurance needed
- Stronger loan application
- Faster time to purchase

## Eligibility Requirements

To use the FHSS, you must:

✓ Be an Australian citizen or permanent resident
✓ Be a first-home buyer (never owned a home)
✓ Be 18 or older
✓ Have a super account
✓ Be using the funds to purchase or construct a home in Australia
✓ Intend to live in the property as your main residence

### What Doesn't Disqualify You?

Importantly, for co-buyers:
- You don't need to be the **only** owner (co-ownership is fine)
- You don't need to live there alone (shared ownership is okay)
- You don't need to own 100% (your percentage ownership doesn't matter)

Each co-buyer can independently access their FHSS amount, as long as each meets the eligibility criteria.

## The Tax Benefits Explained

The FHSS gives you three distinct tax advantages:

### 1. Upfront Tax Deduction
When you make a contribution, you claim it on your tax return:
- $10,000 contribution + $3,000 tax deduction = Net cost $7,000 (at 30% MTR)
- This effectively means the government co-funds 30% of your contributions

### 2. Tax-Free Growth
The money grows tax-free inside your super account:
- No income tax on investment returns
- Usually super earnings are taxed at 15%, so you're saving 15% annually on growth

### 3. Capital Gains Tax Exemption
The gains inside your FHSS account don't trigger CGT:
- Normally withdrawing super early triggers tax
- FHSS withdrawals are completely tax-free
- This is a huge advantage, especially for investment returns

## Comparing to Other Saving Methods

### Traditional Savings Account
- Interest: 4% (taxable if over $18,200/year income)
- Net return: ~2.8% (after tax at 30% MTR)
- Tax on withdrawal: None (but interest already taxed)

### FHSS Strategy
- Super growth: 6% (tax-free inside super)
- Tax deduction benefit: 30% upfront
- Effective return: 7.2% equivalent
- Tax on withdrawal: None

**Winner: FHSS** (especially if you earn above $45,000/year)

## Important Considerations for Co-Buyers

### Point 1: Individual Limits Apply

Each co-buyer has their own $50,000 limit. If you're buying with 4 friends:
- Each person can withdraw up to $50,000
- Total group FHSS available: $200,000
- This is massive for your deposit

### Point 2: First-Home Buyer Status

You must be a first-home buyer. This means:
- You've never owned real estate anywhere in Australia
- This is your first property purchase
- All co-buyers must be first-home buyers to use FHSS

If one co-buyer previously owned property (even in another state), they can't access FHSS, but the others can.

### Point 3: Main Residence Requirement

The property must be:
- In Australia
- Your intended main residence
- Not an investment property
- Owned within 12 months of withdrawal (usually)

If you're buying an investment property with friends, the FHSS won't help.

## The Application Process

### When to Apply

**Timing:** Apply when you're ready to purchase (or have a contract exchanged)

The ATO will want proof that:
1. You're buying a property
2. You're a first-home buyer
3. It's your main residence

### Required Documents

- Copy of the purchase contract or building contract
- Proof of identity
- Your bank account details (for the withdrawal)
- Details of all co-buyers (they each apply separately)

### Timeline

- Application: 1-2 weeks to lodge
- Processing: Usually 1-2 weeks
- Withdrawal: Usually within 5 business days

**Total:** Plan for 3-4 weeks from application to having the funds

## Potential Pitfalls to Avoid

### 1. Withdrawing for the Wrong Reason
The ATO audits FHSS withdrawals. If you withdraw $50,000 and then buy an investment property instead, you'll face a tax bill for the full $50,000 plus penalties.

### 2. Excess Contributions
Once you hit $50,000 designated for FHSS, additional contributions won't be eligible. Plan your contribution strategy.

### 3. Using Spouse's Super Incorrectly
You can only withdraw from your own super. Don't try to withdraw from your partner's account (unless they're also eligible).

### 4. Forgetting About the 12-Month Rule
Some versions of the scheme require you to complete the purchase within 12 months of withdrawal. Check current rules before applying.

### 5. Not Coordinating with Co-Buyers
If you're buying with friends, make sure you all apply at the same time, use the funds for the same property, and keep documentation aligned.

## 2026 Changes to Be Aware Of
